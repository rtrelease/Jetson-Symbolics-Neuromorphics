**Paul Tillich (1886 – 1965)** was a German‑American theologian, philosopher and one of the most influential Protestant thinkers of the twentieth century.  He is best known for developing what he called an “existential theology,” a way of articulating Christian faith that draws on existentialist philosophy, phenomenology and the cultural realities of the modern world.

---

### Early Life & Education  

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **1886** | Born on October 14 in Lüneburg, Germany, to a Protestant pastor. |
| 1903‑1908 | Studied philosophy and theology at the University of Bonn. |
| 1908 | Earned a Ph.D. in philosophy; his dissertation was on *“The Concept of the Right in Kant.”* |

Tillich’s formative years were marked by a deep engagement with German philosophy (Kant, Hegel, Nietzsche) and the early currents of the Frankfurt School, though he remained independent from its more radical politics.

---

### Academic & Professional Career  

| Period | Position / Place |
|--------|-----------------|
| 1910‑1933 | Taught philosophy and theology at universities in Germany (Heidelberg, Hamburg, Kiel). |
| **1933** | Emigrated to the United States to escape the Nazi regime. |
| 1933‑1965 | Professor of Systematic Theology at Union Theological Seminary, New York City (also taught at Yale and Columbia). |
| 1955‑1965 | Served as the *Dean of the Faculty* at Union. |

In the U.S., Tillich became a central figure in American evangelical and mainline Protestant circles, while maintaining an ecumenical openness that attracted scholars from all traditions.

---

### Major Theological Contributions  

1. **The Ground of Being**  
   - Tillich’s most famous idea: *“God is the ground of being”* (Das Wesen des Glaubens).  
   - God is not a being among beings but the underlying reality that makes any being possible.  

2. **The Courage to Be**  
   - A series of essays and books (1960‑1962) in which Tillich turns existential anxiety into a “courageous confidence” in the face of the unknown.  
   - The “courage to be” involves a “faith that is the most authentic being.”  

3. **Existentialism & Theology**  
   - Integrated existentialist themes (choice, freedom, alienation) with Christian doctrine.  
   - Emphasized “ultimate concern” (the deepest level of being that defines a person’s life).  

4. **Theology of Culture**  
   - Works such as *Theology of Culture* (1939) and *The Presence of God in History* (1947) explore how faith interacts with art, politics, science, and social movements.  

5. **Symbolic Theology**  
   - Tillich argued that religious symbols are not literal truths but “symbolic” expressions of deeper realities.  
   - His *Systematic Theology* (1950‑1964, 5 volumes) systematically developed this perspective.

---

| *The Meaning of God* | 1947 | Clarifies the notion of God in modern terms. |

### Influence & Legacy  

- **Theological Reach:** His ideas shaped evangelical and mainline Protestant theology, influenced Catholic theologians (e.g., Karl Rahner), and reached secular thinkers (e.g., Michel Foucault, Hannah Arendt).  
- **Existentialism & Post‑Modernism:** Tillich’s integration of existentialist ideas prefigured post‑modern theological debates on faith and doubt.  
- **Ecumenical Impact:** He bridged Protestant, Catholic, and even Jewish and secular thought, making his work relevant across traditions.  
- **Controversy:** Critics accused him of “denying” God by reducing Him to a ground or concept, while supporters lauded his ability to speak truth to the anxieties of modern life.

---

### Personal Life & Death  

- **Family:** Married to Maria (née von Bülow) in 1930; they had one son.  
- **Death:** Died on September 23, 1965, in New York City after a battle with cancer.  
- **Posthumous Honors:** The Paul Tillich Society, founded in 1972, continues to promote his work.  
- **Archival Materials:** Papers are held at Union Theological Seminary and the Library of Congress.

### Quick Takeaway  

Paul Tillich was a pioneering theologian who sought to make Christian faith intelligible for a modern, secular, and often anxious society. By framing God as the “ground of being” and encouraging believers to develop “the courage to be,” he bridged rigorous philosophy and heartfelt faith, leaving a legacy that continues to inspire theologians, philosophers, and cultural critics today.

## Tillich’s Theology of Anxiety and Angst

Paul Tillich’s work on anxiety (German *Angst*) is one of the most original and enduring contributions of twentieth‑century theology.  It is not an attempt to “cure” fear, but an attempt to **explain what anxiety is, why it is indispensable to authentic existence, and how faith can respond to it**.  Tillich’s insights sit at the intersection of phenomenology, existential philosophy, and Christian symbolism.  

---

### 1.  A Brief Philosophical Context

| Influencer | Key Idea | Tillich’s Response |
|------------|----------|--------------------|
| **Kant** | Freedom as the condition for morality | Tillich re‑interprets freedom as a “choice toward the ground of being.” |
| **Heidegger** | “Being‑toward‑death” creates existential angst | Tillich adopts the phenomenological “courage to be” as the counterpart of Heidegger’s “courage to die.” |
| **Sartre** | Bad faith (*mauvaise foi*) | Tillich argues that bad faith is the refusal to confront ultimate anxiety. |
| **Christian Theology** | God is omnipotent, transcendent | Tillich reduces God to “the ground of being,” a symbolic concept that dissolves the “divine/finite” gap. |

Tillich’s central insight is that **anxiety is not a pathology but the *dialectical tension* between the finite self and the infinite ground of being**.  When a person faces a situation where his or her self‑concept is confronted with a limit (death, loss, the unknown), anxiety arises.

---

### 2.  The Anatomy of Tillich’s Anxiety

#### a.  *Existential Angst* – the fundamental dread

- **Definition**: A universal, deeply felt experience that arises when the self is confronted with its own finitude or uncertainty.  
- **Characteristics**:  
  - Not linked to a specific object or situation; it is *the* feeling of “I am not enough, I am not sure what I am.”  
  - It reveals the *gap* between the self’s finite being and the infinite ground of being.  

#### b.  *Anxiety with a Target* – situational fears

- **Definition**: Fears that are attached to particular objects or circumstances (e.g., fear of losing a job).  
- **Role in the existential picture**: These are **symptoms** or **expressions** of the underlying existential angst.  When the existential anxiety is resolved or accepted, these situational anxieties are often alleviated.

#### c.  *Ultimate Concern* vs. *Ultimate Doubt*

- **Ultimate Concern**: The deepest, most fundamental “ultimate” that drives a person (e.g., love, work, faith).  
- **Ultimate Doubt**: The direct expression of existential anxiety – a doubt that is *not* about a specific fact but about the very possibility of being at all.  
  - Tillich says, *“The ultimate doubt is the anxiety that the ultimate concern must survive.”*

---

### 3.  Anxiety as a Call to the Ground of Being

1. **The Ground of Being**  
   - Tillich’s doctrine of the ground of being identifies God with *“the ground of being”*—the condition that makes existence possible.  
   - Anxiety is the **“breach”** that reveals the ground’s existence; it is the self’s sense that something larger than itself is necessary.  

2. **The Abyss**  
   - Tillich describes the space of anxiety as an *abyss* that separates us from the ground.  
   - The abyss is not a void but a **“space of being”** that is simultaneously open to being and void of being.  
   - *“The abyss is the space that separates us from the ground of being. It is also the space that contains the ground.”*  

3. **The Courage to Be**  
   - Theological response: *“Courage”* is defined as a **theological attitude**—a confidence in the ground of being that persists even when the ultimate doubt arises.  
   - It is *not* the absence of fear; it is the willingness to “be” in spite of the fear.  
   - Tillich presents this in his five‑book *Systematic Theology* (Volumes III–V) and in the *Courage to Be* series (1960–62).

---

### 4.  The Symbolic Dimension of Faith

- **Symbols as a Response**: Faith’s power lies in the *symbolic order* (language, art, rituals) that communicates the truth of the ground.  
- **The Role of Symbolic Truth**: Symbols are *not* literal truths but *transcendent truths* that point to the ground.  
- **Anxiety in Symbolic Language**: The symbolic expression of anxiety (e.g., prayer, lament, confession) is an attempt to *bridge* the finite self to the infinite ground.  
  - In the biblical sense, *“crying out”* is the first step toward communion with God.  
- **Theologizing Angst**: By using the symbolic, we *make sense of* the absurd and give it meaning.

---

### 5.  Practical Implications

| Situation | Tillich’s Insight | Practical Application |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Death** | “Being‑toward‑death” produces existential angst. | Embrace the “courage to be” by affirming the ground of being; engage in sacramental rituals that remind us of the continuity of life. |
| **Loss/Trauma** | Situational anxiety is an expression of ultimate doubt. | Use lamentation and communal rituals to bring the individual into the symbolic order of faith. |
| **Modern Identity Crisis** | Anxiety arises from the conflict between personal values and societal norms. | Reconnect with a “courage” that is anchored in an ultimate concern (community, service, creativity). |
| **Existential Isolation** | The “ground of being” is the connective force that transcends isolation. | Encourage authentic conversations and shared rituals that make visible the underlying ground. |

---

### 6.  Critiques & Legacy

| Critique | Tillich’s Response |
|----------|--------------------|
| **“God is denied”** – critics argue that reducing God to a ground makes God invisible. | Tillich emphasizes that the ground is *symbolically* made visible; the theological “courage” keeps the ground “in the world.” |
| **“Too philosophical”** – the theology is perceived as abstract. | Tillich insists that the philosophical analysis is necessary to bring existential anxiety into the theological conversation; the final product is a living faith. |
| **Ecumenical concerns** – his symbolic theology does not fit neatly into traditional doctrinal frameworks. | Tillich’s openness to “symbolic truth” was meant precisely to allow dialogue among Christian traditions, as well as between faith and secular culture. |

---

### 7.  Take‑away Summary

- **Anxiety = The *dialectic* of finite being and infinite ground.**  
- **Existential angst** is the universal, non‑situational dread that surfaces whenever the self confronts its limits.  
- **Faith’s answer** is not to eliminate anxiety, but to *recognize* it as the invitation to the ground of being.  
- **The “courage to be”** is an act of confidence that bridges the abyss: it is a theological stance that says, “I will *be* in spite of, or because of, this anxiety.”  
- **Symbols** provide the language that makes the invisible ground visible and the experience of anxiety translatable into meaning.

Tillich’s work invites us to see anxiety not as a symptom to be cured but as a *living call* to the ground of being—a call that, if answered with faith, turns the most frightening part of human existence into a profound encounter with the divine.

### Take‑Home Summary  

1. **Recognize angst** as a *gap* between finite self and infinite ground.  
2. **Name the abyss**; make the emptiness visible.  
3. **Invoke the symbolic ground** (God, the ground of being) in language, prayer, or ritual.  
4. **Identify your ultimate concern**—the deepest value that can anchor you.  
5. **Practice the courage to be**: live in presence, trusting that the ground is with you.  
6. **Lean on community** to see the ground symbolically reflected in others.  
7. **Act in a way that honors the concern**, allowing the ground to be lived out.

By following these steps, the *angst* you feel becomes a **gateway**—a living encounter with the ground of being, rather than a problem to be solved.
